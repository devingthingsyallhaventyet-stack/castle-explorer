Eilean Donan Castle

Kyle of Lochalsh 13th Century ★ 4.5

There's a reason this is Scotland's most photographed castle. Perched on a tidal island where Loch Duich, Loch Long, and Loch Alsh converge, Eilean Donan looks like it was placed there by a set designer. It wasn't — Mackenzie chiefs built it in the 13th century to guard against Viking raids. The Royal Navy reduced it to rubble in 1719. What you see today is a love letter: painstakingly rebuilt stone by stone in the early 20th century.

02

Culloden Battlefield

Inverness 1746 ★ 4.6

The last battle fought on British soil lasted less than an hour. On this windswept moor in April 1746, the Jacobite cause — and the Highland clan system — died together. Walking among the clan markers that still stand in the heather is one of the most profoundly moving experiences in Scotland. The NTS visitor centre doesn't just tell you what happened. It puts you there.

03

Urquhart Castle

Loch Ness 1,000+ Years ★ 4.4

Forget the monster — the real draw is the castle. Urquhart has dominated the shores of Loch Ness for over a millennium, changing hands between Scots and English more times than anyone cares to count. Its defenders blew it up themselves in 1692 rather than let it fall to the Jacobites. Today the Grant Tower stands like a broken tooth against the loch, and the views from the ruins are among the finest in Scotland.

04

Dunrobin Castle

Sutherland 13th Century ★ 4.5

You don't expect a French château at the edge of the world. But there it is — 189 rooms of Versailles-meets-Highlands absurdity, complete with formal gardens cascading toward the North Sea. The Earls and Dukes of Sutherland built this over eight centuries, each one outdoing the last. The falconry displays on the lawn are worth the drive alone. The history is darker than the architecture suggests — but that's a story for the visit.

Getting Around

The Highlands demand a car. The North Coast 500 route hits many of the best castles. Single-track roads are part of the charm — use passing places, wave at oncoming drivers, and don't rush. The A9 is your spine; everything branches from there.

📅

When to Go

May–September for long days and (relative) warmth. Castle interiors often close November–March, but ruins don't care about opening hours. Autumn brings stag ruts, golden bracken, and dramatic skies. Midges peak June–August — bring repellent.

🏨

Where to Stay

Several Highland castles double as hotels: Inverlochy, Tulloch, Aldourie on Loch Ness. For budget: Inverness is the hub, with B&Bs everywhere along the NC500. Wild camping is legal in Scotland — the ultimate castle-hopping experience.
