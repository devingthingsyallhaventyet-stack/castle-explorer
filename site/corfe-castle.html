About

Corfe Castle looks like a broken tooth on the Purbeck skyline — shattered towers leaning at impossible angles, walls split clean in half by gunpowder charges. It's the most photogenic destruction in England.

For six hundred years before Parliament got to it, Corfe was a royal castle. King John used it as a treasury and a prison — twenty-two French knights were starved to death in its dungeons. Lady Bankes defended it for three years during the Civil War before being betrayed by one of her own officers.

Now it belongs to the village cats and the National Trust. Climb to the keep and look out across the Isle of Purbeck — the view hasn't changed since William the Conqueror stood here in 1066.

Getting There

🚗 By Car
On the A351 between Wareham and Swanage. National Trust car park in Corfe Castle village (free for members, pay & display for others).
🚂 By Train
Corfe Castle station on the Swanage Heritage Railway is a 5-minute walk. Connect from the main line at Wareham (South Western Railway from London Waterloo).
🚌 By Bus
Purbeck Breezer 40 runs from Wareham to Swanage via Corfe Castle every 30 minutes in summer.
✈️ By Air
Bournemouth Airport (BOH) ~18 miles · Southampton Airport (SOU) ~40 miles

Terrain & Accessibility

⚠️ Steep & Uneven🧗 Rugged Terrain🌬️ Exposed

The castle ruins sit on a steep hill with rough, uneven ground throughout. The climb to the keep is strenuous with no step-free alternative. Not suitable for wheelchairs or pushchairs beyond the outer bailey. Sturdy footwear essential. The village and National Trust tea room at the base are fully accessible.
