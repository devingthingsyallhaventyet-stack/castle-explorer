The Story

On the very edge of Ireland, where the basalt cliffs drop sheer into the Atlantic, a castle clings to the rock like it's daring the sea to take it. Some of it, the sea already has.

The MacDonnells built Dunluce as their stronghold in the 16th century — a fortress so dramatic it looks like a film set. That's because it literally became one. You've seen these walls before: they doubled as the Iron Islands in Game of Thrones, and it's not hard to see why.

Standing here, you're closer to Scotland than Dublin, and closer to a fantasy novel than anything in the modern world.

"Walk through the gatehouse and it opens up: roofless halls with grass floors, window frames that look out at nothing but ocean and sky."

Approach it from the road and the first thing you notice is the gap — a chasm between the mainland and the castle rock, crossed by a narrow bridge with nothing but spray and seabirds below. The Vikings knew this spot. The early Christians before them.

It's the kind of place where you stop taking photos and just stand there.

The Journey

Dunluce sits on the Causeway Coastal Route between Portrush and Bushmills — one of the most scenic drives in the British Isles. Most visitors combine it with the Giant's Causeway, just three miles east.

From Belfast, it's about seventy miles along the stunning Antrim coast road. There's a small car park directly opposite the castle entrance.
