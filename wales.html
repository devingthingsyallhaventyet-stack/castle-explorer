Wales

Wales spent centuries as contested ground between native rulers and a more powerful neighbour. Its own princes built castles, courts, and abbeys to govern a country they were always at risk of losing — and against them came Norman Marcher lords, and finally an English king determined to finish the job for good.

Stories That Shaped Wales

Many Welsh stories come in pairs — a castle built to take the country, and one built to keep it. A route is the best way to see both sides of the same fight standing in the same landscape.

c.1100–1283

The Princes of Wales & Native Welsh Castles

Before the conquest, Wales built castles of its own. This route follows the strongholds of the native princes — Dolwyddelan, Castell y Bere, Dinefwr, Criccieth — and the houses of Gwynedd and Deheubarth who raised them, ruling a country that came within a generation of lasting independence under Llywelyn the Great and his grandson.

1277–1295

Edward I, the Iron Ring & Castles of Conquest

After Wales's last native prince was killed, Edward I made the conquest permanent in stone. This trail runs through the iron ring of Snowdonia — Caernarfon, Conwy, Harlech, Beaumaris — vast fortresses with planted English towns at their feet, built to make sure no Welsh prince would ever rise again.

1400–1415

Owain Glyndŵr & the Last Welsh War of Independence

In 1400 Owain Glyndŵr was proclaimed Prince of Wales and very nearly made it real. His route crosses the country of the revolt — the parliament at Machynlleth, the castles he seized at Harlech and Aberystwyth, and the strongholds that finally broke him — the last great rising for an independent Wales.

1067–1283

The Marcher Lords & the Border Castle Frontier

Along the English border lay the March of Wales, where powerful lords ruled their own private war-zones outside the king's ordinary law. This trail follows their fortresses — Chepstow, Caerphilly, and the line of border castles — built to seize and hold Welsh land for families answerable to almost no one.

c.500–1536

Saints, Abbeys & the Sacred Landscape of Wales

Wales calls itself a land of saints, and the claim is written across the country. This route links the Age of Saints to the cathedral of St Davids, the holy wells and pilgrim paths, and the great Cistercian abbeys like Strata Florida and Tintern — houses that buried princes and helped carry the Welsh language and identity through the conquest.

c.4000 BC–410 AD

Ancient Wales: Tombs, Hillforts & Roman Frontiers

Long before the princes, Wales was already old. This trail runs through Neolithic burial chambers like Pentre Ifan, Iron Age hillforts, the Druids' last stand on Anglesey, and the Roman frontier forts of Caerleon and Segontium — the deep foundations beneath everything that came after.

Before you go

Questions About Exploring Wales's Historic Sites

Wales is small and dense, so let one thread lead you rather than chasing the whole map. A conquest trail through Snowdonia takes in the greatest castles in Britain within a short drive; a Marcher route follows the border fortresses; a saints-and-abbeys route runs west toward St Davids. Distances are short, but the mountains are real — a journey that looks quick on the map can be slow, and spectacular, through the passes.

North Wales, without much argument, for the headline castles: Edward I's iron ring around Snowdonia is the densest concentration of world-class fortresses anywhere. For native Welsh castles, look to the same north and to Deheubarth in the southwest; for Marcher strongholds, the southern and border counties. Few places let you stand at a Welsh prince's castle and an English king's on the same day quite like the north does.

Yes, and the great ones are well looked after. Many of the major castles and abbeys are in the care of Cadw, the Welsh heritage body — some ticketed, some free — and the four Edwardian castles of Gwynedd together form a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Plenty of smaller native castles and ancient sites sit open on hillsides with nothing but a path to them, so think about walking boots as much as opening times.

The main towns and several castle sites — Cardiff, Caernarfon, Conwy, Harlech — are reachable by train, and the coastal and Cambrian lines are scenic in their own right. But the native hill-castles, the abbeys, and the ancient sites are mostly rural, and a car is the difference between seeing the famous fortresses and seeing the country that fought over them.

The essential thread is conquest and resistance: the native princes of Gwynedd and Deheubarth, Edward I's conquest and his iron ring of castles, Owain Glyndŵr's last great revolt, and the Marcher lords along the border — all laid over a far older layer of saints, hillforts, and Roman frontier. Hold that in mind and a Welsh castle stops being just a castle: you can usually tell at a glance whether it was built to hold the country down or to keep it free.
