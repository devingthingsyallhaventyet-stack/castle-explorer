Explore Wales's History

Every siege, conquest, and rebellion left its mark in Welsh stone. These are the events that shaped a nation — and the castles that survived to tell the tale.

78-410 AD

Roman Wales

For three centuries, Rome held Wales with iron discipline. Legions marched from Segontium to Caerleon, building roads, forts, and amphitheatres. When they left, the Welsh princes who emerged would claim descent from both Roman generals and Celtic gods.

1246-1282

Llywelyn the Last

The only Welsh prince ever to unite all of Wales under one crown. Llywelyn ap Gruffudd built alliances, fortresses, and dreams of independence that brought him within a sword's length of making Wales a sovereign nation. His death near Builth Wells ended Welsh independence for seven centuries.

1277-1295

Edward I & The Iron Ring

After crushing Llywelyn the Last, Edward Longshanks built the most sophisticated castle network in medieval Europe to hold Wales forever. The "iron ring" of Caernarfon, Conwy, Harlech and Beaumaris cost more than England's annual revenue and changed castle design across Europe.

1400-1415

Owain Glyndŵr's Revolt

The scholar turned warrior prince who nearly drove the English from Wales. Glyndŵr captured Edward's "impregnable" castles, proclaimed himself Prince of Wales, and held the first Welsh Parliament. Though his revolt failed, he became Wales' greatest folk hero and symbol of independence.

While lords built castles for war, monks built abbeys for God. The Cistercians transformed remote Welsh valleys into centres of learning, agriculture, and breathtaking architecture. Welsh princes patronised them as symbols of native culture — until Henry VIII tore them all down.
