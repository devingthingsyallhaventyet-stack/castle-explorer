East of England

Castles & Historic Sites

Norfolk · Suffolk · Essex · Cambridgeshire · Hertfordshire · Bedfordshire

The East of England is a landscape of wide skies, ancient market towns, and formidable Norman strongholds. From the magnificent keep of Castle Rising in Norfolk to the towering curtain walls of Framlingham Castle in Suffolk, this region's castles tell the story of conquest, rebellion, and royal power. Colchester Castle — built on the foundations of a Roman temple — houses the largest Norman keep ever constructed in Britain, while the elegant Hedingham Castle boasts one of the best-preserved Norman tower keeps in Europe. Along the Suffolk coast, Orford Castle stands as a masterpiece of Henry II's military engineering, its unique polygonal tower still commanding views across the marshes to the sea. Whether you're exploring the Broads, wandering medieval wool towns, or following the coastline from Essex to the Wash, every county here has castles waiting to be discovered.

Getting There

Travelling to the East of England

The East of England is easily reached from London. Norwich is under two hours by train from Liverpool Street, while Colchester is just 50 minutes. Cambridge is less than an hour from King's Cross, making it an ideal base for the western part of the region.

By car, the A11 and A12 are the main arteries from London into Norfolk, Suffolk, and Essex respectively. The M11 provides fast access to Cambridge and beyond. Stansted Airport sits right in the region, with connections across Europe.

Once you're here, a car is recommended for reaching the more rural castle sites. Norfolk and Suffolk's quiet lanes are a pleasure to drive — flat terrain, wide views, and very little traffic outside the main towns. Cycling is also excellent in this famously flat landscape, and many castles sit along well-maintained cycle routes.

Accommodation

Where to Stay in the East of England

Norwich makes an excellent base — a beautiful medieval city with a wide range of hotels and restaurants, and well-positioned for Norfolk's castles. Bury St Edmunds is the perfect hub for Suffolk, a handsome Georgian town with abbey ruins at its heart.

Colchester is ideal for Essex sites, with good rail links back to London. For something more atmospheric, the Suffolk coast towns of Aldeburgh, Southwold, and Woodbridge offer charming B&Bs within striking distance of Orford and Framlingham.

For a castle stay, Haughley Park and various country house hotels across the region let you sleep in genuine historic surroundings. The Norfolk Broads also offer unique houseboat accommodation — explore the waterways by day and visit castles along the shore.

Planning

Best Time to Visit East of England Castles

April to October is the main visiting season when all castle interiors are open and events are running. The East of England is one of the driest parts of Britain, so you'll often enjoy fine weather even when the rest of the country is under cloud.

May and June are particularly lovely — wildflowers carpet the countryside, the Norfolk and Suffolk coasts are uncrowded, and daylight stretches past 9pm. Summer holidays (July–August) bring living history events, jousting displays, and family activities at major sites like Framlingham and Castle Rising.

Autumn is magical in this region. The flat landscapes glow golden in October light, and the skies fill with migrating birds. Most major castles stay open until early November. Winter visits are quieter but rewarding — the big skies and dramatic light make for atmospheric photography, and exterior ruins are accessible year-round.

Frequently Asked

East of England Castle FAQ

How many castles are in the East of England?
The East of England has dozens of castles and historic fortifications spread across Norfolk, Suffolk, Essex, Cambridgeshire, Hertfordshire, and Bedfordshire — ranging from mighty Norman keeps to atmospheric coastal ruins. The exact count on CastleCore is 0, with new sites added regularly.
What is the most famous castle in the East of England?
Framlingham Castle in Suffolk is arguably the region's most iconic site. Its striking curtain wall with thirteen towers is instantly recognisable, and it was here in 1553 that Mary Tudor was proclaimed Queen of England. Colchester Castle, housing the largest surviving Norman keep in Britain, is equally significant historically.
Are East of England castles free to visit?
Several castle ruins in the region are free to visit, including earthwork sites and smaller ruins. Major sites managed by English Heritage — such as Framlingham, Castle Rising, and Orford — charge admission (typically £8–£12 for adults). An English Heritage membership pays for itself after just two or three visits.
What is the best time to visit castles in the East of England?
April to October offers the best experience, with longer daylight hours and all interiors open. The East of England enjoys some of the driest weather in Britain, making it pleasant even in shoulder months. Summer brings living history events, medieval fairs, and jousting at major sites.
Can I visit East of England castles without a car?
Several major castles are accessible by public transport. Colchester Castle is in the town centre, Norwich Castle dominates the city, and Cambridge has good bus connections. However, a car opens up the more remote coastal and rural sites like Orford Castle, Hedingham Castle, and Castle Acre. Local taxi services can fill gaps for car-free visitors.
