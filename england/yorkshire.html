Yorkshire

Yorkshire — England's largest historic county — is a land of wild moors, green dales, and a dramatic coastline, all threaded through with a fierce and fascinating history. The region's 0 castles and historic sites tell tales spanning a thousand years: from the Norman strongholds raised to subdue the rebellious North, through the great abbeys dissolved by Henry VIII, to the grand country houses of the Georgian age. Castle Howard, the baroque masterpiece immortalised in Brideshead Revisited, crowns the Howardian Hills. Skipton Castle, one of the best-preserved medieval castles in England, guards the gateway to the Yorkshire Dales. In Wensleydale, Bolton Castle held Mary Queen of Scots prisoner, while Richmond Castle — one of the oldest stone keeps in the country — watches over Swaledale. On the coast, Scarborough Castle commands its headland and the Gothic ruins of Whitby Abbey loom above the harbour that inspired Dracula. Yorkshire doesn't do things by halves.

Getting There

Travelling to Yorkshire

York is Yorkshire's main transport hub and one of the best-connected cities in England. The LNER East Coast Main Line puts York just 1 hour 50 minutes from London King's Cross, with frequent services throughout the day. Leeds, 25 minutes south of York by rail, has even more connections — it's a major interchange for trains from Manchester, Birmingham, and the South West.

Leeds Bradford Airport and Manchester Airport (90 minutes by car) serve the region for international visitors. Doncaster Sheffield Airport has closed, but Humberside Airport near the coast handles some flights. By car, the A1(M) runs through the heart of Yorkshire from north to south, and the M62 crosses east to west.

Within Yorkshire, a car opens up the Dales and Moors, but many sites are surprisingly accessible by rail. The Settle–Carlisle Railway passes through the western Dales, and the North Yorkshire Moors Railway — a heritage steam line — connects Pickering to Whitby through glorious moorland. York itself is perfect for exploring on foot.

Accommodation

Where to Stay in Yorkshire

York is the obvious base — a spectacular walled city with accommodation from luxury hotels within the medieval walls to budget hostels. You're within easy reach of Castle Howard, the coast, and the Dales. The city's own history — Viking Jorvik, the Minster, Clifford's Tower — makes it a destination in its own right.

In the Yorkshire Dales, Skipton is a practical gateway town with good B&Bs and inns. Deeper into the Dales, villages like Hawes, Leyburn, and Reeth offer charming pub stays and farm cottages. Richmond — one of England's most beautiful market towns — has excellent accommodation right beneath its castle walls.

On the coast, Whitby is a gem: a working fishing port with superb fish and chips, Dracula connections, and abbey views from nearly every street. Scarborough offers classic seaside resort accommodation, from grand Victorian seafront hotels to modern apartments. For the North York Moors, Helmsley and Pickering are picturesque bases with castle ruins of their own.

Planning

Best Time to Visit Yorkshire Castles

April to October is the main season, with all sites open and reliable (if never guaranteed) weather. Yorkshire's diversity means there's always somewhere to explore regardless of conditions — head to the coast on sunny days, and save the atmospheric abbey ruins for misty mornings.

Late August and September bring the famous purple heather bloom across the North York Moors — it's one of England's great natural spectacles and combines perfectly with visits to moorland castles and abbeys. May and June offer long days, wildflowers in the Dales, and fewer crowds than peak summer.

Winter in Yorkshire has real character. York's Christmas market (late November to December) is one of England's best, and the city's medieval streets look magical with festive lights. Many abbey and castle ruins are open year-round and are particularly atmospheric in frost or low winter light. The Dales and Moors can be snow-covered — beautiful but check road conditions before heading out.

Frequently Asked

Yorkshire Castle FAQ

How many castles are in Yorkshire?
Yorkshire has a rich concentration of castles, abbeys, and historic sites spanning from Norman conquest strongholds to grand stately homes. The county's turbulent history — from Viking settlement to the Wars of the Roses — left behind dozens of fortified sites across the Dales, Moors, and coast. The exact count on CastleCore is 0, with new sites added regularly.
What is the most famous castle in Yorkshire?
Castle Howard, the magnificent baroque palace made famous by Brideshead Revisited, is Yorkshire's most celebrated historic house. For medieval castles, Skipton Castle — one of the best-preserved in England, fully roofed and complete — and the dramatic clifftop Scarborough Castle are the standout sites. Richmond Castle's Norman keep is one of the oldest stone castles in the country.
Is Whitby Abbey a castle?
Whitby Abbey is a ruined Benedictine monastery rather than a castle, but it's one of Yorkshire's most important historic sites. Founded in 657 AD, its dramatic Gothic ruins — perched on the East Cliff above Whitby harbour — inspired Bram Stoker's Dracula. It's managed by English Heritage and is well worth combining with nearby Scarborough Castle on a coastal day trip.
Can you visit castles in the Yorkshire Dales?
Absolutely. The Yorkshire Dales are home to several excellent castle sites. Bolton Castle in Wensleydale is a remarkably complete 14th-century fortress where Mary Queen of Scots was imprisoned. Skipton Castle guards the southern gateway to the Dales, and Middleham Castle — Richard III's favourite residence — sits in lower Wensleydale. All three are open to visitors and make for a superb day trip.
What is the best time to visit Yorkshire castles?
April to October offers the best conditions, with all sites open and long daylight hours. The Yorkshire Dales and North York Moors are particularly beautiful in late spring (bluebell season) and early autumn (heather on the moors in August–September). The coast can be visited year-round — Whitby and Scarborough have a moody charm in winter that many visitors prefer to the busy summer months.
