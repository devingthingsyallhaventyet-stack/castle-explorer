Northern England

Castles & Historic Sites

Northumberland · Cumbria · County Durham · Tyne and Wear · Lancashire · Merseyside · Greater Manchester · Cheshire

Northern England is a land forged by conflict — centuries of border warfare between England and Scotland left behind one of the densest concentrations of castles anywhere in Europe. The Northumberland coast alone boasts a staggering chain of fortresses: Bamburgh Castle rising from its basalt crag above sweeping sands, Alnwick Castle — the "Windsor of the North" and Hogwarts stand-in — and the brooding ruins of Dunstanburgh on their lonely headland. Inland, Hadrian's Wall stretches 73 miles across the landscape, the greatest Roman monument in northern Europe. Further south, Carlisle Castle guards the western approaches, Durham Castle stands sentinel beside its magnificent cathedral, and the great houses of Lancashire and Cheshire tell tales of the Wars of the Roses. From 0 sites spanning Roman fortifications to medieval strongholds to Tudor manor houses, Northern England rewards every castle explorer.

Getting There

Travelling to Northern England

Newcastle upon Tyne is the main gateway, with its own international airport (flights from London, Amsterdam, Dublin, Paris, and many European cities) and a major intercity rail station. The LNER East Coast Main Line connects London King's Cross to Newcastle in under 3 hours, with stops at Durham along the way.

Manchester and Liverpool airports serve the western side of the region, with excellent motorway links north via the M6 and M62. Carlisle sits on the West Coast Main Line from London Euston (3.5 hours) and is the starting point for the spectacular Settle–Carlisle Railway across the Pennines.

For the Northumberland coast and Hadrian's Wall, a car is highly recommended. The AD122 bus runs along Hadrian's Wall in summer (Easter to September), but coastal castles are spread out and best explored by car. The A1 runs the length of the region, with the coast road through Alnwick and Bamburgh being one of England's finest drives.

Accommodation

Where to Stay in Northern England

Newcastle offers the widest range of accommodation, from boutique hotels in the Quayside district to budget chains near the station. The city's vibrant nightlife and restaurant scene make it a great base for evening entertainment after a day of castle exploring.

For the Northumberland coast, Alnwick is perfectly positioned — a charming market town with excellent B&Bs, right next to its famous castle. Seahouses and Bamburgh village offer seaside stays within walking distance of the castle. For a unique experience, you can stay on Holy Island (Lindisfarne) — just mind the tide times.

Along Hadrian's Wall, Hexham and Haltwhistle are the main base towns, with country inns, farm B&Bs, and converted barns scattered along the wall corridor. In the west, Carlisle and the Lake District fringe towns offer accommodation ranging from grand country hotels to cosy fell-side cottages.

Planning

Best Time to Visit Northern England Castles

April to September is the prime visiting season. Long summer evenings mean you can fit in multiple castles per day, and all sites are open with full visitor facilities. The Northumberland coast is glorious in summer, though it's exposed — even July days can be breezy.

May and September are the sweet spot: good weather, fewer crowds than peak summer, and everything open. September brings golden light that makes coastal castle photography spectacular. Spring sees puffins arriving at the Farne Islands near Bamburgh — combine castle visits with a boat trip.

Winter has its own appeal. Durham Cathedral and Castle are atmospheric in the low winter light, and Hadrian's Wall dusted with frost is hauntingly beautiful. Many English Heritage sites stay open year-round (check individual sites), and you'll often have the ruins to yourself. Wrap up warm — the Northumberland wind doesn't mess about.

Frequently Asked

Northern England Castle FAQ

How many castles are in Northern England?
Northern England is home to a remarkable concentration of castles, particularly along the Northumberland coast and the Scottish border. From mighty fortresses like Bamburgh and Alnwick to the Roman fortifications of Hadrian's Wall, the region has dozens of historic sites spanning nearly 2,000 years. The exact count on CastleCore is 0, with new sites added regularly.
What is the most famous castle in Northern England?
Bamburgh Castle, perched dramatically on a basalt outcrop overlooking the Northumberland coast, is widely regarded as Northern England's most iconic fortress. Alnwick Castle, used as Hogwarts in the Harry Potter films, is equally famous and draws hundreds of thousands of visitors each year. Durham Castle, a UNESCO World Heritage Site alongside its cathedral, completes the trio of must-see sites.
Can you walk along Hadrian's Wall?
Yes — the Hadrian's Wall Path is an 84-mile National Trail running coast to coast from Wallsend near Newcastle to Bowness-on-Solway in Cumbria. You can walk the entire trail in about a week, or visit individual forts and sections as day trips. The central section between Housesteads and Steel Rigg offers the most dramatic scenery, with the wall snaking across the Whin Sill crags.
Are Northern England castles free to visit?
Many castle ruins and Hadrian's Wall sections are free to explore. English Heritage manages several major sites — an annual membership (around £60 for adults) offers excellent value if you plan to visit three or more properties. Some privately owned castles like Alnwick and Bamburgh charge admission (typically £15–£20) but are well worth the entry fee.
What is the best base for exploring Northern England castles?
Newcastle upon Tyne is the most practical base, with excellent transport links, a wide range of accommodation, and a lively restaurant and pub scene. For the Northumberland coast castles (Bamburgh, Dunstanburgh, Warkworth), the town of Alnwick is ideal. For Hadrian's Wall, Hexham or Haltwhistle put you right on the trail with good B&B options.
