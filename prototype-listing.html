About

Edinburgh Castle sits on a 350-million-year-old volcanic plug, and it shows. The rock rises sheer on three sides, leaving only one approach — a narrow ridge from the east that became the Royal Mile. People have fortified this spot since at least the Iron Age, making it one of the longest continuously occupied sites in Britain. The castle you see today is mostly 16th and 17th century, but St Margaret's Chapel, tucked inside the walls, dates to around 1130 and is the oldest surviving building in Edinburgh.

The Honours of Scotland — crown, sceptre, and sword of state — are the oldest crown jewels in Britain, predating the English set by a century. They sit in the Crown Room alongside the Stone of Destiny, which spent 700 years at Westminster Abbey before being returned to Scotland in 1996. Mons Meg, the six-tonne medieval siege cannon on the battlements, once launched a stone cannonball two miles across the Firth of Forth at Dumbarton Castle.

Every day at exactly one o'clock, the Time Gun fires from the Half Moon Battery — a tradition since 1861, originally so ships in the Forth could set their chronometers. It's the most besieged place in Great Britain, having been attacked 26 times in its 1,100-year history. In 1314, thirty Scots disguised as merchants scaled the sheer north face in darkness, climbing a route so treacherous it had been left unguarded. By dawn, the castle was theirs.

Terrain & Accessibility

📐 Steep Approach ⚠️ Cobblestones 🦽 Mobility Vehicle Available 🌬️ Exposed & Windy

The Royal Mile approach is a steep cobbled climb. A free courtesy vehicle runs from the Portcullis Gate to the summit for visitors with mobility difficulties — book at the ticket office. Wheelchairs available to borrow. Crown Jewels and Great Hall are fully accessible; some areas including St Margaret's Chapel involve steps.

Getting There

🚗 By Car

There is no parking at the castle. The nearest car parks are Castle Terrace (NCP) and St James Quarter. Approach via the A1 or M8/M9 into the city centre — follow signs to the Old Town.

Get driving directions →
🚂 By Train

Edinburgh Waverley is a 10-minute uphill walk via the Mound or Playfair Steps. Haymarket station is 20 minutes on foot. Frequent services from London King's Cross (LNER, ~4.5 hrs), Glasgow Queen Street (ScotRail, ~50 min), and all major Scottish cities.

🚌 By Bus

Lothian Buses routes 2, 23, 27, 41, and 42 stop on George IV Bridge or The Mound, both a short walk from the castle esplanade. The hop-on-hop-off tour buses stop directly at the castle entrance.

✈️ Nearest Airports

Edinburgh Airport (EDI) is 8 miles west — the Airlink 100 bus reaches the city centre in 30 minutes. Glasgow Airport (GLA) is ~50 miles.

History

c. 900 BC
First occupation of Castle Rock

Archaeological excavations reveal the volcanic rock was artificially levelled for habitation during the late Bronze Age. The site's sheer cliffs on three sides make it a natural fortress, with only one approach from the east.

c. 600 AD
Din Eidyn — Gododdin fortress

The stronghold appears in Y Gododdin, an Old Welsh poem describing warriors feasting at Din Eidyn before marching south. A timber and earthwork fort tops the rock, likely the capital of the Votadini tribe. It falls to the Northumbrian Angles in 638 AD.

c. 1130
St Margaret's Chapel built

David I builds a small Romanesque chapel in honour of his mother, Queen Margaret, along with a Norman-style castle keep — the first stone fortification on the rock. The chapel, just 5.5m by 3.2m, is the oldest surviving building in Edinburgh.

1296
Captured by Edward I

During the Wars of Scottish Independence, Edward I besieges the castle for three days with 10,000 men. The garrison of fewer than 100 surrenders. The Stone of Destiny is removed to Westminster Abbey.

1314
Recaptured and razed to its foundations

Thomas Randolph leads 30 men up the sheer north face at night to recapture the castle. Robert the Bruce then orders the entire fortress demolished down to bedrock — ensuring the English can never use it again.

1370s
David's Tower constructed

David II rebuilds the castle with a massive new tower rising 30 metres above the rock — the tallest structure the castle has ever seen. It houses the royal chambers and becomes the heart of the rebuilt fortress for the next century.

1430s
James I adds the Great Chamber

A second tower is built behind David's Tower, containing a large hall purpose-built for banquets and state occasions. The royal apartments shift here, and Crown Square begins to take shape as the ceremonial heart of the castle.

1460s
Crown Square completed

James III creates the Grand Parade (now Crown Square), a formal courtyard modelled on continental royal residences. The castle transforms from a medieval fortress into something closer to a palace. Mons Meg, the six-tonne siege cannon, arrives as a gift from the Duke of Burgundy in 1457.

c. 1510
The Great Hall completed

James IV builds a grand new Great Hall with a medieval open-timber roof — the only surviving example in Scotland. Oak beams are imported from Norway. The hall hosts the Scottish Parliament and state banquets.

1566
Birth of James VI

Mary, Queen of Scots gives birth to the future James VI in a tiny chamber in the Royal Palace — barely 3 metres square. It is the castle's first and last royal birth. The room can still be visited today.

1573
The Lang Siege — David's Tower destroyed

English cannon bombard the castle for nearly three years. David's Tower collapses under the barrage. In the aftermath, the massive semicircular Half Moon Battery is built over its ruins, armed with bronze cannons known as the 'Seven Sisters'.

1790s
New Barracks constructed

With the castle now primarily a military garrison, the New Barracks are built to house soldiers. The Great Hall is subdivided into three storeys as a barracks, then repurposed as a military hospital. St Margaret's Chapel is used to store ammunition.

1818
Honours of Scotland rediscovered

Sir Walter Scott leads a search and discovers the Scottish crown jewels in a sealed room, untouched since 1707. They had been hidden after the Act of Union to prevent removal to London.

1897–1927
Restoration and the War Memorial

The Great Hall is restored by architect Hippolyte Blanc, returning it closer to its medieval appearance. In 1927, the Scottish National War Memorial is added, designed by Sir Robert Lorimer — the last major building constructed within the castle walls.

1996
Stone of Destiny returns

After 700 years at Westminster Abbey, the Stone of Destiny is formally returned to Scotland and placed on display alongside the Honours of Scotland in the Crown Room.

People

St Margaret
c. 1045 – 1093
Queen consort of Scotland. Died in the castle; the oldest building on site — St Margaret's Chapel — was built in her honour. Canonised in 1250.
Robert the Bruce
1274 – 1329
King of Scots. Ordered the castle's defences destroyed after Thomas Randolph recaptured it in 1314, to prevent English reuse.

Thomas Randolph

Sir Walter Scott
1771 – 1832
Novelist and poet. Led the 1818 search that rediscovered the Honours of Scotland — the crown jewels — locked in a sealed room, untouched since 1707.

Architecture & Construction

The castle complex spans multiple periods. The oldest structure is St Margaret's Chapel (c. 1130), a simple Romanesque chapel. The Great Hall, completed in 1511 for James IV, features Scotland's only surviving medieval open-timber roof. The Half Moon Battery (1573–1588) was constructed over the ruins of David's Tower after the Lang Siege. The Scottish National War Memorial was added in 1927, designed by Sir Robert Lorimer.

Designations
