West Wales

Castles & Historic Sites

Pembrokeshire · Carmarthenshire · Ceredigion

West Wales stretches along the wild Pembrokeshire coast, through the rolling hills of Carmarthenshire, and up into the quiet uplands of Ceredigion. This region is home to 50 castles, abbeys, and ancient sites — from the mighty Pembroke Castle, birthplace of the Tudor dynasty, to the dramatic cliff-edge ruin of Carreg Cennen and the brooding towers of Kidwelly Castle. Whether you're walking the Pembrokeshire Coast Path, exploring the Neolithic burial chamber of Pentre Ifan, or discovering Laugharne Castle where Dylan Thomas found his inspiration, West Wales weaves together Welsh princes, Norman invaders, and thousands of years of history.

Getting There

Travelling to West Wales

West Wales is well served by road from the M4 motorway. Carmarthen is the main gateway town, around 2 hours from Cardiff and 4 hours from London by car. The A40 and A48 are the main routes west from the M4.

By train, Great Western Railway and Transport for Wales run services to Carmarthen, Tenby, Pembroke Dock, and Fishguard from Swansea and Cardiff. The journey from London Paddington to Carmarthen takes around 4 hours. Fishguard also has ferry connections to Rosslare in Ireland.

Once in West Wales, a car is strongly recommended. The Pembrokeshire coast has excellent roads but public transport between castle sites is limited. The Pembrokeshire Coastal Bus services are useful in summer for reaching coastal sites, but inland castles like Carreg Cennen require your own transport.

Accommodation

Where to Stay in West Wales

Tenby is the most popular base — a beautiful walled town on the Pembrokeshire coast with excellent hotels, guesthouses, and restaurants. It's well positioned for Pembroke Castle, Manorbier, and Carew.

St David's, Britain's smallest city, offers a quieter base near the cathedral and the wild northern Pembrokeshire coast. Carmarthen and Llandeilo are ideal for exploring the Carmarthenshire castles, with Llandeilo particularly well placed for Carreg Cennen and Dinefwr.

For character, try a converted farmhouse or coastal cottage — Pembrokeshire is famous for its holiday lets. Camping and glamping sites dot the coast path, and the region has some excellent independent hostels for budget travellers.

Planning

Best Time to Visit West Wales Castles

May to September is the best time to visit. The Pembrokeshire coast is at its finest in late spring when wildflowers carpet the cliff-tops and puffins nest on Skomer Island. Summer brings the warmest weather and longest days, though popular spots like Tenby can be busy in August.

April and October are excellent shoulder months. The coast is quieter, autumn colours brighten the Carmarthenshire valleys, and most Cadw sites remain open. Spring is particularly magical for the bluebells in the woodlands around Cilgerran and the Teifi Valley.

Winter (November–March) brings dramatic storm-watching on the coast and peaceful castle ruins without the crowds. Some castle interiors close or reduce hours, but ruins and outdoor sites are accessible year-round. West Wales has milder winters than inland Wales thanks to the Gulf Stream.

Frequently Asked

West Wales Castle FAQ

How many castles are in West Wales?
There are around 50 castles, ruins, abbeys, and historic sites across West Wales, spanning from Neolithic burial chambers to mighty Norman fortresses. The exact count on CastleCore is 50, though new sites are added regularly.
What is the most famous castle in West Wales?
Pembroke Castle is the most famous — a massive Norman fortress and birthplace of Henry VII, founder of the Tudor dynasty. Carreg Cennen, spectacularly perched on a limestone crag in Carmarthenshire, is equally iconic and often considered Wales's most dramatically sited castle.
Are West Wales castles free to visit?
Many castle ruins in West Wales are completely free to explore. Cadw, the Welsh heritage body, manages several sites with free or low-cost entry. Major paid sites like Pembroke Castle and Kidwelly Castle typically charge £5–£10 for adults. A Cadw membership offers unlimited access to all their sites.
Can I walk the Pembrokeshire Coast Path between castles?
Yes! The 186-mile Pembrokeshire Coast Path passes near several castles and historic sites. You can walk between Manorbier Castle and Pembroke Castle along the coast, or visit Cilgerran Castle from the Teifi estuary section. The path is well-marked and offers some of the finest coastal walking in Britain.
What else is there to see in West Wales besides castles?
West Wales offers stunning beaches, the Pembrokeshire Coast National Park, St David's Cathedral (Britain's smallest city), wildlife boat trips to Skomer and Ramsey islands for puffins and seals, the Preseli Hills (source of Stonehenge's bluestones), and the charming towns of Tenby, Laugharne, and Cardigan.
