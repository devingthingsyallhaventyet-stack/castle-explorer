Snowdonia & North Wales

Castles & Historic Sites

Gwynedd · Conwy · Denbighshire · Flintshire · Wrexham · Anglesey

Snowdonia & North Wales is the heartland of Welsh castle building, where Edward I planted his mighty Iron Ring of fortresses to control a conquered nation. From the imperious towers of Caernarfon Castle — where the first English Prince of Wales was presented — to the geometric perfection of Beaumaris on Anglesey, this region holds 49 castles, ruins, and historic sites. Beyond the UNESCO-listed royal fortresses, you'll find native Welsh strongholds like Dolbadarn and Criccieth, dramatic hilltop ruins such as Dinas Brân above Llangollen, and medieval town walls still encircling Conwy. All set against the spectacular backdrop of Snowdonia's mountains, coastline, and ancient valleys.

Getting There

Travelling to Snowdonia & North Wales

North Wales is well connected from England's motorway network. The A55 Expressway runs along the north coast from Chester to Holyhead, passing within minutes of Conwy, Beaumaris, and Caernarfon. From Manchester or Liverpool, the drive takes around 1.5–2 hours.

By train, Transport for Wales runs the scenic North Wales Coast Line from Chester through Rhyl, Colwyn Bay, and Llandudno Junction to Bangor and Holyhead. The Conwy Valley Line branches south to Betws-y-Coed and Blaenau Ffestiniog — one of Wales's most beautiful rail journeys. Direct trains from London Euston reach Bangor in around 3.5 hours via Crewe.

The nearest airport is Liverpool John Lennon or Manchester, both around 90 minutes' drive. Once in North Wales, a car is highly recommended — while the coastal castles are train-accessible, Snowdonia's mountain castles and rural ruins require your own transport.

Accommodation

Where to Stay in North Wales

Caernarfon is the ideal base for castle explorers — the town itself is dominated by its mighty fortress, and Beaumaris, Dolbadarn, and Criccieth are all within easy reach. The walled town of Conwy is equally atmospheric, with the castle literally on your doorstep.

Betws-y-Coed in the heart of Snowdonia makes a great base for combining castles with mountain walks. Llandudno offers a more traditional seaside-town experience with excellent Victorian hotels. Llangollen in the Dee Valley is perfect for the eastern castles.

For something special, Bodysgallen Hall near Llandudno is a 17th-century country house hotel with views to Conwy Castle and Snowdonia. Several castles in the region also offer holiday cottage accommodation within their grounds.

Planning

Best Time to Visit North Wales Castles

April to September is the best period — all Cadw-managed castles are open daily, the weather is warmest, and daylight hours are long. Summer brings festivals and re-enactment events at several castles.

May and September are the sweet spots: warm enough for comfortable exploring, fewer crowds than July–August school holidays, and lower accommodation prices. Snowdonia's mountains are at their best in late spring with clear skies.

Winter (November–March) sees reduced opening hours at major sites, but the castles remain atmospheric — Harlech against a stormy sea or Dolbadarn dusted with snow are unforgettable sights. Ruins with no gates, like Dinas Brân, can be visited year-round.

Frequently Asked

North Wales Castle FAQ

How many castles are in Snowdonia & North Wales?
There are 49 castles, ruins, and historic sites across Snowdonia & North Wales on CastleCore, including four UNESCO World Heritage castles built by Edward I.
What is the Iron Ring of castles?
The Iron Ring refers to the chain of formidable castles built by Edward I of England in the late 13th century to subdue Wales after his conquest. The key fortresses are Caernarfon, Conwy, Harlech, and Beaumaris — all UNESCO World Heritage Sites and among the finest medieval military architecture anywhere in Europe.
Which is the best castle to visit in North Wales?
Caernarfon Castle is the most impressive and historically significant, built as a deliberate symbol of English power. Conwy Castle offers the best combined experience with its intact town walls. Harlech has the most dramatic clifftop setting, and Beaumaris is considered the most technically perfect concentric castle ever built.
Are North Wales castles free to visit?
Many ruined castle sites are free to visit, including hilltop ruins like Dinas Brân and Dolbadarn. The major Cadw-managed castles — Caernarfon, Conwy, Harlech, Beaumaris, Criccieth — charge admission, typically £8–£14 for adults. A Cadw Explorer Pass offers good value if visiting multiple sites.
Can I visit Snowdonia castles without a car?
The main coastal castles are accessible by public transport — Caernarfon, Conwy, and Harlech are on or near rail lines, and Beaumaris is reached by bus from Bangor. However, a car opens up Snowdonia's mountain castles and the more remote ruins in Denbighshire and Wrexham.
