Mid Wales & the Marches

Castles & Historic Sites

Powys

Mid Wales & the Marches is the quiet heart of Welsh castle country — a landscape of rolling green hills, river valleys, and ancient borderlands where Welsh princes and English Marcher Lords fought for control across centuries. The county of Powys stretches from the Brecon Beacons in the south to the Berwyn mountains in the north, and holds 19 castles and historic sites. The jewel is Powis Castle near Welshpool, a medieval fortress transformed into one of Britain's finest country houses. Beyond it lie atmospheric ruins like Dolforwyn Castle — the last castle built by a native Welsh prince — the literary town of Hay-on-Wye with its castle, the fortified manor of Tretower Court, and lonely hilltop strongholds like Castell Dinas high above the Usk Valley.

Getting There

Travelling to Mid Wales & the Marches

Mid Wales is rural and wonderfully uncrowded, but that means transport requires a little planning. By car, the A483 from Shrewsbury runs south through Welshpool and Newtown, while the A470 is the main north–south spine through the heart of Wales. From Birmingham, the drive to Welshpool takes around 1.5 hours; from Cardiff, Brecon is about an hour via the A470.

By train, the Cambrian Line from Shrewsbury to Aberystwyth stops at Welshpool, Newtown, and Caersws — one of Wales's most scenic rail routes. From London, change at Shrewsbury (around 4 hours total). The Heart of Wales Line from Swansea to Shrewsbury passes through southern Powys with stops at Llanwrtyd Wells, Builth Road, and Knighton.

Once in Powys, a car is essential. Distances between sites are significant, and rural bus services are infrequent. The reward is quiet roads through some of the most beautiful and least-visited countryside in Britain.

Accommodation

Where to Stay in Mid Wales

Welshpool is the best base for the northern castles — a pleasant market town right next to Powis Castle, with good pubs, B&Bs, and easy access to the Severn Valley. Hay-on-Wye is perfect for the southern sites, offering characterful bookshop-town charm alongside excellent restaurants and accommodation.

Brecon is ideal if combining castles with walks in the Brecon Beacons National Park (now Bannau Brycheiniog). The town has a ruined castle of its own, a fine cathedral, and a good selection of inns and guesthouses.

For something special, the region has excellent farmhouse B&Bs and converted barn stays — Mid Wales is where you come to slow down, and the accommodation reflects that. Llangoed Hall near Brecon, a country house designed by Clough Williams-Ellis, is the luxury option.

Planning

Best Time to Visit Mid Wales Castles

April to October is the main season — Powis Castle and Tretower Court are open, the weather is mild, and the valleys are at their greenest. The Hay Festival in late May brings literary visitors and a buzzing atmosphere to Hay-on-Wye.

Late spring (May–June) is ideal: long daylight, wildflowers in the valleys, and far fewer visitors than the popular coastal regions. Autumn (September–October) brings spectacular colours to the Wye and Usk valleys.

Winter is quiet but atmospheric. Powis Castle's gardens are beautiful in frost, and the open-access ruins can be visited year-round. Roads are generally fine, though hill passes can be icy. The Brecon Beacons sometimes see snow, making for dramatic castle backdrops.

Frequently Asked

Mid Wales Castle FAQ

How many castles are in Mid Wales & the Marches?
There are 19 castles, ruins, and historic sites across Mid Wales & the Marches on CastleCore, concentrated along the ancient border between Wales and England.
What is the best castle to visit in Mid Wales?
Powis Castle near Welshpool is the undisputed highlight — a medieval fortress transformed into a grand country house with world-class gardens, stunning interiors filled with treasures, and a fascinating Clive of India museum. It's managed by the National Trust.
What were the Welsh Marches?
The Welsh Marches were the borderlands between England and Wales, controlled by powerful Marcher Lords who were granted extraordinary authority by the English crown. These lords built castles to defend and expand English territory, creating a contested frontier that produced some of Britain's most atmospheric castle ruins.
Are Mid Wales castles free to visit?
Many ruined castles in Powys are free to visit, including Dolforwyn, Castell Dinas, and Builth Castle mound. Powis Castle charges National Trust admission (free for members). Tretower Court is managed by Cadw with a modest entry fee.
Is Mid Wales easy to get to?
Mid Wales is rural and best explored by car. The A483 and A470 are the main routes. Welshpool and Newtown have rail stations on the Cambrian Line from Shrewsbury. From the Midlands or Shrewsbury, the drive into Powys takes around an hour.
