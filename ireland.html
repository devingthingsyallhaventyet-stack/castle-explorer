Ireland

For most of its history Ireland held two worlds at once: the Gaelic Ireland of clans, brehon law, and cattle-wealth, ruled by lords like the O'Neills and O'Briens; and the colonial Ireland of the walled town, the king's writ, and the imported castle — overlapping and fighting for centuries without either fully swallowing the other.

Stories That Shaped Ireland

Ireland's history is best read in layers, and a route is the cleanest way to dig down through them. One trail follows a single people; another follows a single century when one world gave way to the next.

c.3200 BC – 400 AD

Ancient Ireland: Tombs, Royal Hills & Sacred Landscapes

Long before writing reached Ireland, its people shaped the land itself. This route runs through passage tombs older than Stonehenge, the royal inauguration hill of Tara, stone circles, and ritual landscapes laid out to the sun and the seasons — the sacred geography of an Ireland we know only through what it built.

c.500 – 1100

Saints, Scholars & Monastic Ireland

While much of Europe fell dark, Ireland's monasteries kept learning alight. This trail links the great monastic cities — Glendalough, Clonmacnoise, the sea-rock of Skellig Michael — through their round towers, high crosses, and the scriptoria that sent Irish monks back across Europe as missionaries.

1169 – 1300

The Anglo-Norman Invasion & Medieval Lordship of Ireland

The Norman arrival of 1169 planted a colony that never quite conquered the whole island. Its route runs from the first beachheads through mighty keeps like Trim, the walled towns, and the Pale around Dublin — and into the country where Norman families slowly turned Irish, "more Irish than the Irish themselves."

1400 – 1600

Gaelic Lords, Clan Castles & the Tower-House World

Beyond the colony, the Gaelic and gaelicized lords ran a world of their own. This trail follows their thousands of tower houses — the fortified homes of families like the O'Briens and MacCarthys — and the cattle-wealth, poetry, and law that sustained a late-medieval Gaelic revival the colony could never absorb.

c.1530 – 1603

Grace O'Malley & the Pirate Queen's Atlantic

From the castles of Clew Bay, Grace O'Malley — Granuaile — ran a sea-lordship of galleys, tolls, and raids along the western coast. Her route traces a Gaelic maritime power at the edge of Europe, and a chieftain who sailed to London to bargain, face to face, with Elizabeth I.

1565 – 1607

The Tudor Conquest, Nine Years' War & the End of Gaelic Ireland

This is the story of how the two Irelands collapsed into one. The route follows the Tudor reconquest, the great rising of O'Neill and O'Donnell, the disaster at Kinsale, and the Flight of the Earls — the moment the Gaelic order lost its leaders, its land, and the country to the plantations that followed.

Before you go

Questions About Exploring Ireland's Historic Sites

Pick a layer of the past rather than a postal route. Ireland packs an enormous span of history into a small island, so a single thread carries you furthest — the deep prehistory and monasteries of the Ancient East, a Gaelic tower-house trail through the southwest, or the plantation story across Ulster. The whole island is drivable in a matter of days, but it rewards going slowly through one chapter at a time.

It depends which castle you mean. For Norman power, the east and Munster hold the great keeps and walled towns — Trim, Cahir, the Rock of Cashel. For the Gaelic tower-house world, the southwest and west are thick with them. Ulster has the plantation castles and dramatic coastal ruins like Dunluce, while Dublin anchors it all with its castle at the heart of the old colony.

Yes, and access is unusually open. Many of the great sites — Brú na Bóinne, the Rock of Cashel, Glendalough — are run by the Office of Public Works, some ticketed and some free, and a remarkable number of smaller ruins, monastic sites, and tower houses sit in fields you can simply walk up to. Remote western and island sites depend on weather and, for places like Skellig Michael, on boats that only run in season, so check ahead.

For the cities — Dublin, Cork, Galway, Belfast — public transport is fine, and the trains between them are comfortable. But Ireland's history is overwhelmingly rural: the tombs, monasteries, and tower houses that make the island distinctive sit down country lanes and along the coast, and a car (or a guided tour) is what gets you to them.

A few threads carry most of it: the deep prehistoric and monastic ages that have no real parallel next door in Britain, the Anglo-Norman invasion that planted a colony, the parallel Gaelic world of the tower-house lords, and the Tudor conquest and plantations that finally broke it. Hold those in mind and a round tower, a tower house, and a star-shaped fort stop looking similar and start telling you exactly which Ireland you're standing in.
