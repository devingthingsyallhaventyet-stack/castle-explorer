Explore Northern Ireland's History

From ring forts to Norman strongholds, every conquest and rebellion left its mark on Ulster's landscape. These are the epochs that shaped the Six Counties — and the monuments that survived to tell the tale.

500 BC – 1177

The Gaelic Kingdoms of Ulster

Before the Normans, Ulster was ruled by Gaelic kings from Navan Fort — the legendary Emain Macha. The Ulaid, the Dál Fiatach, and the great O'Neill dynasty held power across a landscape of ring forts, crannógs, and round towers for over a thousand years.

1177 – 1400

The Norman Conquest of Ulster

John de Courcy stormed into Ulster in 1177 with just 22 knights and seized Downpatrick. Within decades, the Normans had built Carrickfergus, Dundrum, and Greencastle — the great stone fortresses that still dominate the coastline.

1609 – 1700

The Plantation of Ulster

After the Flight of the Earls, the English Crown planted Ulster with English and Scottish settlers. New towns, fortified bawns, and plantation castles rose across the landscape — Enniskillen, Monea, Tully — reshaping Ulster's character forever.

The Anglo-Irish Ascendancy built grand country houses across Ulster — Castle Coole, Castle Ward, Florence Court. When Ireland was partitioned in 1921, Northern Ireland's castles became monuments to a divided history.
